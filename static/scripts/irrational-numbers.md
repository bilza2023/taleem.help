Alright… today we’re not memorizing a definition.
We’re going to understand what rational and irrational numbers actually *are*.

Let me start with a question.

When I write 1/2, what is that?

Most students say “a fraction.”

But think deeper. What is a fraction really?

It’s a comparison.
It’s saying: take 1 unit, divide it into 2 equal parts.

That’s a relationship between two whole numbers.

Now here’s the key idea:

A rational number is any number that can be written as a ratio of two integers.

Pause.

Ratio of two integers.

Integers mean whole numbers — positive, negative, or zero.
But the denominator cannot be zero. Because division by zero is not allowed.

So 3/4 is rational.
-7/5 is rational.
10 is rational — because 10 is actually 10/1.
Even -12 is -12/1.

Now let’s go deeper.

What about decimals?

Is 0.5 rational?

Yes. Because 0.5 = 1/2.

What about 0.75?

Yes. 0.75 = 3/4.

So terminating decimals are rational.

Now here comes something interesting.

What about 0.3333333… forever?

Many students think that’s different.

But no.

0.3333 repeating is exactly equal to 1/3.

So repeating decimals are rational too.

In fact, here is a powerful truth:

Every rational number, when written as a decimal, either
• terminates
or
• repeats in a pattern.

Always.

Now let’s pause and ask something important.

Why does repetition happen?

Because when you divide one integer by another, the remainders eventually repeat.
And when remainders repeat, digits repeat.

That repetition is not random. It is structural.
It happens because there are only limited possible remainders.

So rational numbers are predictable.
They are structured.
They come from clean ratios.

Now let’s shift.

What is an irrational number?

An irrational number is a number that cannot be written as a ratio of two integers.

That’s it.

Not “complicated number.”
Not “long decimal.”
Not “big number.”

Just: cannot be written as a/b where a and b are integers.

Now the big example:

√2.

Can √2 be written as a fraction?

No.

And this is not just a claim. It has a proof.

The proof shows that if you assume √2 = a/b, you reach a contradiction.
You are forced to conclude both a and b are even.
But if both are even, then the fraction was not in simplest form.
Which contradicts the assumption.

So √2 cannot be expressed as a ratio.

That’s why it is irrational.

Now let’s talk about decimals again.

What does √2 look like as a decimal?

1.41421356237…

Does it terminate? No.
Does it repeat in a pattern? No.

And here’s the second big truth:

Irrational numbers have decimal expansions that never end
and never repeat in a pattern.

Never.

So now we see a clear difference:

Rational → ratio of integers → decimal terminates or repeats.
Irrational → not a ratio → decimal never ends and never repeats.

But I want to go even deeper.

Imagine the number line.

Between 0 and 1, how many rational numbers are there?

Infinite.

You can always make smaller fractions:
1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/5…

Even between 1/2 and 1/3, there are infinitely many more.

So rational numbers are dense.

Now here’s something shocking.

Irrational numbers are also infinite.
In fact… there are more irrational numbers than rational numbers.

Way more.

Even though rational numbers feel “normal,”
most real numbers are actually irrational.

π is irrational.
√3 is irrational.
e is irrational.

So the number line is not mostly fractions.

It is mostly numbers that cannot be written as fractions.

Let that sink in.

Now let’s connect emotionally.

Why did humans even discover irrational numbers?

Because of geometry.

Take a square with side length 1.

What is the length of its diagonal?

By Pythagoras:

√(1² + 1²) = √2.

And that diagonal length cannot be written as a fraction.

So the Greeks were shocked.

They believed everything was ratio.

And suddenly geometry produced something that was not a ratio.

That discovery changed mathematics forever.

So here’s the clean mental picture I want students to leave with:

Rational numbers are numbers built from whole-number relationships.

Irrational numbers are numbers that cannot be captured by any such relationship.

One comes from division of integers.

The other escapes that structure.

Both live on the same number line.

Both are real numbers.

But only one can be expressed as a clean ratio.

Now let me end with a powerful summary:

If you can write a number as a fraction of integers → rational.

If you cannot → irrational.

If its decimal ends or repeats → rational.

If its decimal never ends and never repeats → irrational.

And finally:

Rational numbers are about comparison.
Irrational numbers are about limitation of comparison.

That’s the full story.
